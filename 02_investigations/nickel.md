# B1 — Electrode Metal Stock (Nickel wire / mesh / foil)

**BoM item:** B1 — Electrode metal stock (wire / mesh / foil) for KrCl excimer lamp electrodes
**Date:** May 2026
**Material investigated:** Nickel (Ni), with notes on aluminium as an alternative

---

## Description

Nickel is a silvery transition metal with strong corrosion resistance, particularly in halogen-containing environments. For KrCl 222 nm excimer lamps, nickel (or nickel-plated metal) is the preferred electrode metal because the Cl₂/KrCl gas fill is highly corrosive — bare refractory metals, hafnium-coated metals, and nickel-plated metals are specifically cited in patent literature as halogen-resistant electrode candidates (US patent literature on non-oxidising electrode arrangements). In practice, electrode form varies by lamp geometry: research-grade coaxial DBD lamps frequently use aluminium foil for the inner electrode and metallic tape as the outer ground electrode; commercial designs (e.g. Ushio Care222) employ nickel or nickel-alloy mesh or foil where chlorine exposure must not degrade electrode lifetime or contaminate the gas fill. Electrode-grade nickel is typically Nickel 200 (N02200) or Nickel 201 (N02201), with ≥99.0% Ni purity, drawn into wire and woven or expanded into mesh or cut as foil. This grade is classified as Class I nickel and trades at a premium to the lower-purity Class II nickel pig iron (NPI) used in bulk stainless steel production.

---

## Confidence and completeness

⚠️ Moderate overall. Mine and refined production totals are well-sourced (USGS MCS 2024/2025, Nornickel AR). Application-breakdown figures (stainless, batteries, alloys, electroplating) are robust at the major-segment level. The share of nickel consumed as electrode-grade wire/mesh/foil for UV and specialty lamps is not reported by any public source and must be estimated from indirect reasoning — the figure is very small relative to total production. The Class I vs Class II distinction matters here: lamp electrodes require Class I, and this sub-market has different supply dynamics from the bulk nickel market.

### Derivation: Nickel in far-UV lamp electrodes

No source publishes this number. The following is an order-of-magnitude derivation:

- A coaxial DBD excimer lamp electrode (inner + outer) uses a small amount of metal — on the order of **1–20 g of nickel-grade metal per lamp unit** (estimated from literature dimensions: foil ~0.1 mm thick × a few cm² area × Ni density 8.9 g/cm³). ⚠️ This range is based on physical reasoning from described lamp geometries, not a measured specification.
- Global installed base of 222 nm excimer lamps is in the tens of thousands of units (2024), with annual production estimated in the low-to-mid thousands of new units. Even assuming 100,000 lamps/year × 20 g Ni/lamp = **2 tonnes/year** of electrode-grade nickel. ❓
- The wider UV lamp market (germicidal UV, industrial photochemical) also uses nickel electrodes, but this is still a tiny fraction. An upper-bound estimate of all UV specialty lamp electrode nickel globally: **< 100 tonnes/year**. ❓
- At 3.5 Mt global refined consumption, 100 t = **0.003%** of total demand.

This places lamp electrodes firmly within measurement noise for global nickel statistics. The relevant constraint for far-UV lamp production is not global nickel supply — it is the availability and lead time of specialty-fabricated, high-purity (Ni200/Ni201) wire, mesh, or foil cut to lamp specifications.

## Producers and geographic concentration

Global nickel mine production is heavily concentrated in Indonesia, which accounted for approximately 59–61% of world output in 2024 (~2.2 Mt), up from 48% in 2022. The next largest producers are the Philippines (~9%, 330 kt), Russia (~6%, 210 kt), Canada (~5%, 190 kt), Australia (~3%, 110 kt), and New Caledonia (~3%, 110 kt). For refined Class I nickel (the grade relevant to electrode wire/mesh), the supply picture differs: Nornickel (Russia) is historically the largest single producer of Class I refined nickel at ~200–220 kt/yr; Vale (Brazil/Canada) produces ~160 kt/yr; Jinchuan (China) and Sumitomo Metal Mining (Japan) are also significant. Indonesia's surge has primarily been in Class II (NPI and MHP), which requires further processing to reach Class I purity. The electrode mesh/foil fabrication step is performed by a wide range of specialty metal processors in China, India, the US, and Europe, drawing on Class I feedstock; this tier of the supply chain is not concentrated.

## Potential supply considerations

- **Indonesia dominance and regulatory risk.** Over half of global mine supply is now in a single jurisdiction. Indonesia has periodically changed nickel ore export rules and mining quota policies; supply disruptions in 2024 temporarily moved LME prices. The trajectory is toward continued Indonesian growth, but regulatory risk is present.
- **Russia sanctions and Class I supply.** Nornickel remains the world's leading Class I nickel producer, but Western sanctions and trade restrictions following the Ukraine conflict have disrupted supply flows. This affects the premium end of the market where electrode-grade nickel sits. US import sources show Russia share declining (Canada 46%, Norway/Finland rising).
- **Class II → Class I conversion capacity.** Indonesia's surplus is Class II. Converting to Class I (battery-grade sulfate or pure metal) requires additional capital-intensive refining steps. This capacity is being built in China, but the Class I market remains tighter than aggregate nickel statistics suggest.
- **Market surplus and price.** The nickel market ran consecutive surpluses in 2023 and 2024, with LME cash prices declining from ~$25,800/t (2022) to ~$17,000/t (2024). Low prices caused multiple producers in Australia, Philippines, and New Caledonia to reduce or halt output. A sustained low-price environment could reduce long-term supply development.
- **Specialty fabrication lead times.** For lamp-electrode applications the binding constraint is likely fabrication-to-specification (mesh weave, foil thickness, purity certification) rather than upstream nickel availability. Multiple qualified suppliers exist, but custom specifications require qualification and may have lead times of weeks to months.

## Alternative materials

- **Aluminium (Al) foil/tape.** Used in laboratory/research DBD excimer lamps (e.g. Bidawat et al. 2023 coaxial design). Suitable when electrode is external to the discharge (DBD geometry, no direct Cl₂ contact). Not suitable for internal electrodes in contact with the gas fill. Al global supply is vastly larger and less concentrated than nickel. ✅ If a specific lamp design uses external-only Al electrodes, the upstream supply chain reverts to aluminium, not nickel.
- **Molybdenum (Mo) and tungsten (W).** Refractory metals with high halogen resistance. Used in some excimer laser electrodes. Higher cost and more limited fabrication ecosystem for thin mesh/foil. Relevant for high-power or long-life lamp designs.
- **Hafnium-coated metals.** Cited in patent literature as halogen-resistant. Hf coating over Ni or Mo substrate. Introduces hafnium supply chain considerations (see HfO₂ investigation for Hf background).
- **Stainless steel (316L or similar).** Moderate Cl₂ resistance; used in some UV lamp designs where purity requirements are lower. Cheaper and more widely available than nickel, but lower purity and different corrosion characteristics.

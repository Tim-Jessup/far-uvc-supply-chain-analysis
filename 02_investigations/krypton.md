## Purified Krypton (Kr) Gas

**Parent BoM item:** C1 — Gas fill for KrCl 222 nm excimer lamp (Stage C)
**Date:** May 2026
**Status:** Research complete

**Description.** Krypton is a noble gas present in air at ~1.1 ppm (0.00011%). It is recovered exclusively as a coproduct of large-scale air separation units (ASUs) via cryogenic distillation, where it is co-produced with xenon and purified to ≥99.999% (5N grade) for electronics and excimer applications. Krypton's principal commercial uses are insulating gas fills in double/triple-pane windows, halogen incandescent lamp fills, and high-purity gas for excimer lasers and semiconductor plasma etching. In KrCl excimer lamps, krypton is the dominant fill gas (typically >95% of the gas mix by partial pressure), combined with a small fraction of chlorine donor (Cl₂ or HCl).

---

### Confidence and completeness

Overall data quality is weak-to-moderate. Primary production statistics for krypton are not published by governments or trade bodies; production data are not covered by USGS Mineral Commodity Summaries for krypton directly. The most authoritative public source is the 2022 EU JRC briefing (based on DERA/Elsner 2018), which gives a 2017 reference figure for crude neon and notes that pure krypton volume is roughly one-third of pure neon. Application breakdown data are largely from commercial market research firms with opaque methodologies and wide internal disagreement. Far-UV lamp-specific krypton consumption is not separately tracked by any source and must be estimated from lamp geometry and fill-pressure specifications.

### Global annual production

- **Best guess:** ~120,000 m³ (450 t) of purified krypton ⚠️
- **Range (low / high):** ~75,000 m³ (280 t) / ~200,000 m³ (750 t)
- **Reference year:** 2017 (most authoritative figure); scale may have shifted post-2022
- **Method(s):**

**Method 1 — JRC/DERA derivation (indirect):**
The JRC 2022 briefing states (citing DERA/Elsner 2018) that global crude neon output in 2017 was ~720 million litres, yielding ~360 million litres of pure neon. It also states that "the production volume of pure neon is larger by around three times compared to krypton." This implies pure krypton production ≈ 360M litres ÷ 3 = **120 million litres = 120,000 m³** at standard conditions (STP). At a krypton gas density of 3.749 kg/m³ (STP), this is approximately **450 tonnes**. ⚠️ (derived via two steps from a single secondary source; the "3x" ratio is approximate)

**Method 2 — Market value cross-check:**
Multiple market research reports estimate the global krypton market at $200–650 million USD (range reflects definitional differences; lower end likely covers only purified grades for specialty use). At reported spot prices of ~€0.10/litre (2018 German import price per JRC) = ~$110/m³, a market of $200M implies ~1.8M m³; at the 2022 war-spike price of ~$5/litre = $5,000/m³, a $500M market implies ~100,000 m³. The wide spread reflects price volatility and market scope uncertainty; the lower volume figure ($500M ÷ $5,000/m³ ~100,000 m³) is roughly consistent with Method 1. ❓

Methods converge loosely on 100,000–150,000 m³/year for traded purified krypton, but the base data are weak. Total crude krypton generated in ASUs globally is much higher; only a fraction is purified and sold.

**Key assumptions:**
- "Production" = commercially purified and traded krypton, not total crude krypton cycling through ASUs
- The "3x neon" ratio from DERA is accepted as approximate; variance could push estimates to the range limits
- 2017 figure assumed not dramatically different from 2024 on the supply side, though post-2022 disruption and geographic reshuffling have altered producer shares
- Krypton gas density at STP: 3.749 kg/m³ ✅

**Far-UV lamp consumption derivation (bottom-up):**

KrCl DBD excimer lamps operate with krypton as the dominant fill gas. Published patent specifications suggest fill pressures in the range of ~0.2–1.0 atm partial pressure of Kr, with Cl₂ source at <1% partial pressure, in a sealed tube volume. For a representative lamp geometry (envelope ~15 mm OD × 150 mm active length ≈ 26 mL internal volume at operating conditions), and a fill pressure of 0.2 atm Kr:

- Kr at STP per lamp ≈ 0.2 atm × 0.026 L = ~0.005 L = 5 mL per lamp ❓

Even at 1 million lamps/year (an aspirational large-scale production figure well above current market), total krypton consumed would be ~5,000 m³/year — roughly 4% of estimated global production. At current far-UV lamp market scale (likely tens of thousands to low hundreds of thousands of units globally), the figure would be ~50–500 m³/year, a negligible fraction of global output. ❓

These are order-of-magnitude estimates only; actual lamp design volumes and fill pressures vary by manufacturer and product.

### Producers and geographic concentration

Krypton production is structurally tied to the location of large ASUs (oxygen capacity >1,000 t/day), which are predominantly co-located with steelmaking plants. China has the largest share of crude Kr/Xe-capable ASUs globally (~33%) and the largest share of Kr/Xe purification plants (~44%), making it the dominant production geography. The global industrial gas majors — Linde, Air Liquide, Air Products, and Messer — dominate purification and distribution, with operations across China, the US, Germany, France, and other steel-producing regions. Ukraine was historically a major purifier (~40% of global krypton supply before 2022), leveraging Soviet-era ASU infrastructure; facilities at Mariupol (Ingas) and Odessa (Cryoin) ceased operations following Russia's 2022 invasion. Russia has its own crude production but export access was constrained by sanctions. Post-2022, Chinese and Western European producers have been the primary sources for displaced demand. The JRC reports ~10% of world ASUs for crude Kr/Xe are in the EU (primarily Germany), with Linde, Air Liquide, Nippon Gases, and BASF operating EU purification facilities.

### Potential bottlenecks

- **Steel industry co-dependence:** Krypton availability tracks steel production (and oxygen demand), not lamp or semiconductor production. Steel sector downturns can reduce crude rare gas output independently of krypton demand.
- **Ukraine production loss:** Pre-2022, Ukraine supplied ~40% of global krypton. The Mariupol and Odessa facilities are unlikely to resume operations at prior scale while the conflict continues. Geographic diversification is underway but adds lead time of 1–3 years per new purification plant.
- **Russia export restrictions:** Russia imposed export restrictions on noble gases from mid-2022; the extent to which these remain in force affects supply to non-Western buyers.
- **Purity qualification lead times:** Changing krypton suppliers for excimer lamp use requires re-qualification of purity to ≥99.999%; process qualification can take months.
- **Price volatility:** Krypton's small market size makes it susceptible to sharp price movements when supply-demand balance shifts even slightly. The 2022 war caused ~50% krypton price rises; the 2014–2015 neon crisis precedent shows rare gas prices can rise 10–20× before the market rebalances. These spikes do not reflect physical scarcity so much as thin spot markets.
- **Add capacity slowly:** New rare gas recovery capability can only be added to very large existing ASUs; new ASU construction is capital-intensive (multi-year lead time).

---

### Alternative materials

For the KrCl excimer fill specifically, krypton has no substitute as the rare gas host. The 222 nm emission arises from the KrCl* exciplex; other rare-gas-halide excimer transitions (XeCl at 308 nm, KrF at 248 nm, ArF at 193 nm) produce different wavelengths. Argon or xenon cannot replace krypton for the 222 nm application. In other krypton end-uses, argon is a cheaper substitute for window fills (with lower insulating performance) and LEDs increasingly substitute for krypton-halogen lighting — but neither substitution relieves demand for the excimer lamp application.

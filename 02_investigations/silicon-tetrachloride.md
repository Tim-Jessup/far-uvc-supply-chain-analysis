## Ultra-High-Purity Silicon Tetrachloride (SiCl₄)

**BoM position:** A1 — primary chemical precursor for synthetic fused silica envelope fabrication  
**Parent BoM item:** A2 — Synthetic fused silica soot preform (via flame hydrolysis / VAD/OVD)  
**Analysis date:** 2026-05-29

**Description.** Silicon tetrachloride (SiCl₄, tetrachlorosilane; CAS 10026-04-7) is a colourless, volatile liquid (b.p. 57.7°C) produced either by direct chlorination of ferrosilicon/silicon carbide or as a byproduct of polysilicon manufacturing. Its largest end uses are polysilicon production (internal/recycled), optical fibre preform deposition, fumed silica, and — at much smaller volumes — high-purity fused silica for optical and UV applications. For far-UVC lamp envelopes (Suprasil 310 or equivalent), ultra-high-purity SiCl₄ (≥99.9999%, 6N metals-grade, free of H-containing impurities such as HSiCl₃) is oxidised by flame hydrolysis to yield a synthetic fused silica soot preform: SiCl₄ + 2H₂O → SiO₂ + 4HCl. Stoichiometrically this requires **2.83 kg SiCl₄ per kg of SiO₂ produced** (MW ratio 169.90/60.09). Alternative precursors exist (OMCTS for the direct-quartz process route) but SiCl₄-based flame hydrolysis dominates premium UV-grade production.

### Global annual production

- **Best guess:** ~1.3 million metric tonnes/year ⚠️
- **Range (low / high):** ~900 kt / ~1.6 Mt
- **Reference year:** 2024
- **Method(s):**  
  - *Market report figure:* Multiple market research reports converge on ~1.3 Mt for 2024, with market value of ~$2.6 billion (implied ~$2,000/tonne average).  
  - *Cross-check via polysilicon:* Global polysilicon production reached ~1.7 Mt in 2024 (Bernreuter Research). The Siemens process generates ~4–16 tonnes of SiCl₄ per tonne of polysilicon, but the vast majority is recycled internally to trichlorosilane. Net SiCl₄ that exits the polysilicon loop and enters other markets is a much smaller fraction, estimated at ~10–20% of gross generation. This implies a few hundred kt/year of polysilicon-byproduct SiCl₄ available externally. Intentional ferrosilicon chlorination accounts for the balance (fumed silica and optics applications do not use polysilicon-byproduct streams because of purity concerns). These two streams together are roughly consistent with the ~1.3 Mt figure, though that figure likely includes some double-counting of recycled internal flows.  
  - *Convergence:* Weak. The 1.3 Mt figure is sourced entirely from market reports with no visible primary data. The cross-check supports an order-of-magnitude range of 1–2 Mt but cannot pin it tightly.
- **Key assumptions:**  
  - The 1.3 Mt figure counts both externally marketed SiCl₄ and some recycled polysilicon-process material; the true "consumed" volume for fused silica/optical fibre applications is likely 200–400 kt/year net
  - No distinction between technical, electronic, and fibre grade in the headline figure

**Far-UVC lamp envelope sub-segment (derived estimate):**  
No public data exists for SiCl₄ consumption attributable specifically to UV-lamp-grade synthetic fused silica. A rough bound can be constructed:

- A typical KrCl excimer lamp envelope (Care222-class) is a fused silica tube weighing approximately 50–200 g.
- At 2.83 g SiCl₄ per g SiO₂, one envelope requires ~140–570 g of SiCl₄.
- Even at 10 million lamp units/year (a very large hypothetical scale), total SiCl₄ demand would be ~1,400–5,700 tonnes/year — roughly **0.1–0.4% of global SiCl₄ production** at current scale.
- At current far-UVC lamp production volumes (likely tens to hundreds of thousands of units/year globally in 2024–2026), the SiCl₄ demand for lamp envelopes is in the range of a few tonnes per year. ❓

This analysis focuses on SiCl₄ supply to the **synthetic fused silica soot preform** step (A2), which is where SiCl₄ is converted to SiO₂ via flame hydrolysis. The relevant bottleneck question is therefore not global SiCl₄ supply (ample) but whether 6N-grade SiCl₄ with appropriate purity for UV optics is accessible from qualified suppliers in adequate quantities.

### Producers and geographic concentration

The overall SiCl₄ market is moderately concentrated: the top five producers control approximately 58% of global supply. Key players include **Evonik** (Germany; produces Siridion® STC 200, used in fibre and semiconductor applications), **Tokuyama** (Japan; electronics-grade; ~45% of fibre-grade market jointly with Evonik), **Wacker Chemie** (Germany), **DOW** (USA), **Linde** (industrial gases, distribution), **Shin-Etsu** (Japan), **OCI** (South Korea), and a growing cluster of Chinese producers including **Shandong Xinlong Group**, **Tangshan Sunfar Silicon**, and **Grinm Guojing Advanced Materials**. Asia-Pacific accounts for over 65% of total SiCl₄ production capacity, with China alone responsible for over 60% of optical fibre production capacity (and correspondingly, a large share of fibre-grade SiCl₄ demand). For ultra-high-purity UV-optics grade, the qualified supplier pool is much narrower: Evonik and Tokuyama are the principal western suppliers; Chinese producers are scaling but may not yet meet the sub-ppb metallic-impurity specifications required for Suprasil-class fused silica.

The synthetic fused silica producers who consume UV-grade SiCl₄ (Heraeus, Tosoh, Corning, Shin-Etsu, Momentive, AGC) may produce or closely specify their SiCl₄ supply rather than purchasing commodity-grade material.

### Potential supply chain considerations

- **Purity threshold is the key constraint, not volume.** Global SiCl₄ production is large relative to far-UVC lamp demand. The binding question is whether 6N-grade, H-impurity-free SiCl₄ is available from qualified suppliers in quantities consistent with scaled lamp production.
- **Vertical integration at fused silica producers.** Heraeus and Tosoh (the dominant UV-grade synthetic fused silica producers) may purify SiCl₄ in-house or under long-term supply arrangements, reducing exposure to spot-market availability. The degree of this integration is not publicly disclosed.
- **Geographic concentration in Asia.** Expanding UV-lamp production outside Asia may face lead times for qualifying Asian-origin SiCl₄ for western UV-grade fused silica plants, or vice versa.
- **Polysilicon byproduct dynamics.** SiCl₄ byproduct from solar-grade polysilicon production is large-volume but not directly usable for optical/UV-grade fused silica without extensive purification; any scenario in which polysilicon production shifts significantly (e.g., trade policy) would alter gross SiCl₄ supply without directly helping UV-optics producers.
- **China supply dependency.** Chinese producers increasingly supply fibre-grade SiCl₄ at competitive prices. Geopolitical disruption or export controls on advanced semiconductor precursors could affect availability of high-grade material outside China, though SiCl₄ is not currently under specific export restrictions.
- **Scaling path is gradual.** Any new entrant to 6N-grade SiCl₄ production faces significant capital investment and qualification timelines (years, not months) to reach the purity levels needed for UV-optical fused silica.

---

### Alternative materials

- **OMCTS (octamethylcyclotetrasiloxane):** An alternative precursor for synthetic fused silica via the direct-quartz (DQ) process. Avoids chlorine chemistry entirely but results in a low-OH material; not suitable for high-OH UV lamp envelopes (which require ~200 ppm OH for radiation resistance). ❓ Suitability for Care222-class lamps: not confirmed.
- **TEOS (tetraethyl orthosilicate):** Used in some sol-gel and CVD processes; generally not used for UV-grade optical fused silica due to residual carbon contamination risk.
- **No practical substitute exists** for SiCl₄ in the flame-hydrolysis route to high-OH UV-grade synthetic fused silica (e.g., Suprasil 310).

### Open questions / follow-ups

1. **What purity specification does the SiCl₄ need to meet for Suprasil 310 / equivalent UV-grade fused silica?** Specifically: what are the maximum allowable concentrations of HSiCl₃, metal impurities, and OH-precursor species? Heraeus likely specifies this internally.
2. **Do Heraeus, Tosoh, and other UV-grade fused silica producers source SiCl₄ externally or produce it themselves?** This determines whether the supply chain is effectively two steps or one.
3. **What is the global capacity for UV-grade / optical-grade (as opposed to fibre-grade or semiconductor-grade) SiCl₄?** The fibre-grade market is ~$343M; the UV-optics sub-segment is not separately tracked.
4. **How much SiCl₄ is actually consumed (net, not recycled) per tonne of synthetic fused silica in a production setting?** The stoichiometric minimum is 2.83 kg/kg, but process efficiency, off-cuts, and soot preform yield losses may increase effective consumption.
5. **What are the qualification lead times and minimum order quantities for 6N SiCl₄ from western vs. Asian suppliers?** This is relevant to supply risk for a scaled far-UVC lamp manufacturer that is not co-located with a fused silica supplier.
